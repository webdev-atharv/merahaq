What is the Right to Information — and Why It Matters

Passed in 2005, the Right to Information Act is one of the most powerful tools an ordinary Indian citizen has against government corruption and bureaucratic silence. Under this law, any citizen can ask any public authority for information — documents, reports, expenditure records, inspection notes, anything — and the government is legally bound to respond within 30 days.

The practical uses are enormous. People have used RTI to find out why their pension was stopped, track missing ration cards, check if their plot's construction permits are legal, find out why a tender was awarded to a specific contractor, and even expose corruption in local panchayats. It is not just a legal right — it is a lever for accountability.

Yet most Indians never file one, either because they think it is complicated or because they don't know where to start. It is not complicated. The entire process takes about 20 minutes.

Who Can File an RTI?

Any Indian citizen can file an RTI. There is no age restriction, no income requirement, and no educational qualification needed. You do not need a lawyer, an agent, or a "fixer". The law was deliberately written to be accessible to everyone — from a PhD holder to a daily wage worker who suspects their MGNREGA payments are being diverted.

  • Citizens only: RTI is a right for Indian citizens. Companies, NGOs, and foreigners cannot file under this Act (though NRIs can, as Indian citizens).
  • No reason required: You are not required to explain why you want the information. The government cannot ask you to justify your request.
  • BPL cardholders are fully exempt: If you hold a Below Poverty Line card, the ₹10 fee is waived. Attach a photocopy of your BPL card with the application.

✅ Important: RTI covers all central government ministries, state government departments, local bodies, PSUs, universities receiving government funds, and even some private bodies that perform public functions. If public money is involved, RTI likely applies.

How to File RTI Online — Step by Step

The Government of India's official RTI portal at rtionline.gov.in lets you file applications for all central government ministries and departments without leaving your home. This is the fastest and most traceable method.

  1. Go to rtionline.gov.in and register Click "Submit Request" on the homepage. First-time users need to register with a valid email address. Your login credentials are sent to your email immediately.
  2. Select the ministry or department Choose the relevant central ministry from the dropdown. If you're not sure which department handles your issue, select the parent ministry — they are required to transfer it internally. For state governments, check your state's RTI portal separately (e.g., Maharashtra RTI, UP Jansuvidha).
  3. Write your information request Keep it specific and factual (more on how to write a good RTI below). You can type directly in the form. Keep it under 500 words for clarity.
  4. Pay the ₹10 fee online Pay via net banking, debit card, credit card, or UPI. A payment receipt is generated — download and save it. BPL cardholders can upload their BPL card certificate instead.
  5. Submit and note your Registration Number After submission, you receive a registration number. Use this to track your application's status on the portal anytime.

💡 Tip: After filing, check your application status every 5–7 days on the portal. If the status stays at "Transferred" for more than 10 days without an update, the department may be delaying unnecessarily — note the dates carefully for your appeal.

How to File RTI by Post or In Person

For state government departments, local bodies, panchayats, and offices that are not on the central RTI portal, offline filing is your route. It is equally valid and enforceable by law.

  1. Write your application on plain paper No special form or stamp paper is required. Write in Hindi or English — both are acceptable. Use our free RTI Letter Generator → to create a properly formatted letter in seconds.
  2. Address it to the Public Information Officer (PIO) Every government office has a designated PIO by law. Address the letter: "To, The Public Information Officer, [Full Department Name], [Complete Address]". If you don't know the PIO's name, that's fine — addressing the designation is sufficient.
  3. Attach a ₹10 Indian Postal Order (IPO) Buy an IPO at any post office. Make it payable to the Accounts Officer of the department you are writing to. If you hold a BPL card, attach a photocopy instead — no IPO needed.
  4. Send by registered post or submit in person Registered post gives you a tracking receipt and proof of delivery. If submitting in person, insist on a stamped acknowledgement with date — do not leave without it.
  5. Keep copies of everything Retain: a copy of your application, the registered post receipt, the IPO counterfoil, and any acknowledgement received. These become essential if you need to file an appeal later.

After Filing — The RTI Timeline

Understanding what happens next is important so you know when the government is on time, when it's late, and when it's breaking the law.

Day 0
You submit your RTI application and pay the ₹10 fee. The clock starts from the day the PIO receives it — not the day you sent it.
Day 1–5
If sent to the wrong department, they must transfer it to the correct one within 5 working days and notify you. The new department then gets a fresh 30 days.
Day 30
Legal deadline for reply. The PIO must either provide the information, ask for more fees (for photocopies etc.), or give written reasons for refusal. Silence is a deemed refusal and grounds for appeal.
No reply?
File a First Appeal with the First Appellate Authority in the same department. It is free. Must be filed within 30 days of the 30-day deadline passing. The authority has 30–45 days to decide.
Still no reply?
File a Second Appeal with the Central or State Information Commission. Also free. The Commission can impose a penalty of ₹250 per day of delay on the PIO, up to ₹25,000, and recommend disciplinary action.

⚠️ Critical: If information involves the life or liberty of a person (e.g., a missing person in police custody, a prisoner's status), the reply must come within 48 hours. If it doesn't, file an appeal immediately — courts have treated delays in such cases very seriously.

How to Write an RTI That Actually Gets Answered

The quality of your RTI application directly affects the quality of the reply. A vague or poorly framed question gives the PIO room to give a vague or unhelpful answer. Here is what experienced RTI filers — including journalists and activists — recommend:

Ask for documents, not explanations

Instead of writing "Why was my ration card cancelled?", ask for "Certified copies of all documents, orders, and noting on file related to the cancellation of ration card No. [XXXXX] in the name of [your name]." Documents are harder to dodge than questions. They reveal the actual decision-making trail.

Be specific about what you want

Name exact dates, amounts, project names, or file numbers wherever possible. "What is the total expenditure on road repair in Ward No. 14 between April 2024 and March 2025, with contractor details?" will get a far more useful response than "What money was spent on roads?"

One RTI, one subject

If you have questions about three different things — say, your pension, a local tender, and a land record — file three separate applications. Bundling them gives a department a reason to delay or partially respond. Each focused application is harder to dismiss.

You don't need to explain yourself

Many first-time filers feel they need to justify why they want the information. You don't. Section 6(2) of the RTI Act explicitly states that you are not required to give a reason for seeking information. Leave the "reason" field blank or write "Not applicable as per Section 6(2) of RTI Act 2005".

Language and format

Write in simple, clear language. Hindi is perfectly acceptable — most state RTI portals and post offices handle Hindi applications daily. Avoid emotional language or complaints — stick to factual requests. Think of it as a formal letter, not a grievance.

💡 Practical tip: End your application with: "I request you to provide the above information within the statutory period of 30 days as provided under Section 7(1) of the RTI Act 2005. In case of any additional fee required, please inform me in writing." This signals to the PIO that you know the law and are tracking the timeline.

What Information Can and Cannot Be Requested

RTI is powerful, but it has defined limits. Section 8 of the Act lists exemptions. Understanding these prevents frustration and helps you frame requests that won't hit dead ends.

You can generally ask for:

  • Government expenditure records, contractor details, tender documents
  • Copies of government orders, policies, and circulars
  • Information about schemes — who received benefits, how much, when
  • Status of your own application or file with any department
  • Inspection reports, audit reports, survey data
  • Details of government employees' service records (with some limits)

Information that can be withheld:

National SecurityInformation that would harm India's sovereignty, security, or strategic interests
Cabinet NotesDiscussions in Cabinet meetings until a decision is formally announced
Personal PrivacyPrivate information about a third party that has no public interest connection
Ongoing InvestigationsInformation that would impede prosecution or police investigations
Commercial ConfidenceTrade secrets or intellectual property that third parties shared in confidence
Foreign Government InfoInformation received in confidence from a foreign government

✅ Even if information falls under an exemption, it must be disclosed if the public interest in disclosure outweighs the harm caused. This is called the "public interest override" and has been successfully used in many RTI cases.

The Appeal Process — When the Government Ignores You

Filing an appeal is not aggressive or unusual — it's part of the system. Thousands of first appeals are filed every month. Most PIOs take applications more seriously when they know the applicant understands the appeal mechanism.

First Appeal

If you receive no reply in 30 days, or if you receive an unsatisfactory or incomplete reply, file a First Appeal with the First Appellate Authority (FAA) in the same department. This is a senior officer designated for this purpose. The appeal must be filed within 30 days of the deadline or within 30 days of receiving an unsatisfactory reply. There is no fee.

Address it to: "The First Appellate Authority, [Department Name]". Attach copies of your original application, proof of submission, and if you received a response, a copy of that too. State clearly what information was not provided and why you are appealing.

Second Appeal — Central/State Information Commission

If the first appeal is also unsatisfactory or ignored, you can go to the Central Information Commission (CIC) for central government departments, or the State Information Commission (SIC) for state departments. File within 90 days of the first appeal decision (or failure to decide). This too is free.

The Information Commission has real powers — it can order information to be disclosed, impose penalties on the PIO, and recommend disciplinary proceedings. Many important RTI cases have been decided at this level, and the CIC's decisions are publicly available online.

💡 Track CIC hearings and file your second appeal at cic.gov.in. You can attend the hearing in person or submit a written statement. The process is more formal than the first appeal but completely doable without a lawyer.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the fee for filing an RTI application? ›
The filing fee is ₹10. For offline applications, pay via Indian Postal Order (IPO), demand draft, or court fee stamp. For online filing at rtionline.gov.in, pay via net banking, debit/credit card, or UPI. BPL cardholders are fully exempt — attach a photocopy of your BPL card instead of the fee.
How many days does the government have to reply to my RTI? ›
30 days from the date the PIO receives your application. If the matter involves someone's life or personal liberty, the reply must come within 48 hours. If your application was transferred to another department, the new department gets another 30 days from the transfer date.
What can I do if there is no reply within 30 days? ›
File a First Appeal with the First Appellate Authority in the same department — this is free and must be filed within 30 days of the deadline. If the First Appeal is also ignored or unsatisfactory, file a Second Appeal with the Central or State Information Commission, which can impose a penalty of ₹250 per day (up to ₹25,000) on the PIO.
Can I file an RTI anonymously? ›
Not completely — you must provide a name and mailing address for the government to send the reply. However, you are not required to explain why you want the information, and you don't need to provide a phone number or email. If privacy is a concern, some applicants use initials instead of a full name, though this may occasionally complicate delivery of the reply.
Can an RTI application be rejected? ›
Yes, but only for specific reasons listed in Section 8 of the Act (national security, ongoing investigations, personal privacy, etc.). A rejection must always come in writing with stated reasons — a PIO cannot simply stay silent. If you believe a rejection is unjustified, that itself is grounds for a First Appeal.
Does RTI apply to private companies? ›
RTI directly applies only to public authorities — government bodies and organizations substantially funded by the government. Fully private companies are not covered. However, if a private body performs a public function or receives substantial government funding, it may fall within RTI's scope. Courts have applied RTI to BCCI, privately managed schools receiving government aid, and similar entities.
