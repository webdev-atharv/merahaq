⚖️ Overview

Why Every Indian Must Know Their Legal Rights

The Constitution of India guarantees powerful fundamental rights to every citizen — rights that cannot be taken away by any government, court, or individual. These rights are enforceable in court. If they are violated, you can approach the High Court or Supreme Court directly. But rights only protect you if you know they exist.

Millions of Indians face illegal arrests, discrimination, denial of wages, forced eviction, and exploitation every day — often because they don't know they have the right to resist. This guide covers your six fundamental rights, your rights when arrested, your right to free legal help, and your right to information from the government.

📜 Fundamental Rights

The 6 Fundamental Rights — Guaranteed by the Constitution

Part III of the Indian Constitution (Articles 12–35) guarantees 6 fundamental rights to all citizens. These apply regardless of religion, caste, gender, wealth, or literacy level.

1. Right to Equality (Articles 14–18)

Every Indian is equal before the law. The government cannot discriminate on the basis of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth in any matter. Untouchability is constitutionally abolished — practising it is a criminal offence under the Protection of Civil Rights Act. No government-given titles like "Rai Bahadur" or "Sir" are permitted.

💡 What This Means in Practice A hospital cannot refuse treatment to a patient based on caste. An employer cannot refuse to hire someone based on religion (with limited exceptions). A government school cannot bar admission based on a child's community. If any government authority discriminates against you, it is challengeable in court.

2. Right to Freedom (Articles 19–22)

Every citizen has the right to: free speech and expression, peaceful assembly without weapons, forming associations and unions, moving freely throughout India, residing in any part of India, and practising any profession or trade. Article 21 specifically guarantees the right to life and personal liberty — no person can be deprived of life or liberty except by procedure established by law.

The Supreme Court has expanded Article 21 to include: right to privacy (2017 Puttaswamy judgment), right to a dignified life, right to livelihood, right to clean environment, right to health, and right to education (though education is now separately guaranteed under Article 21A for ages 6–14).

3. Right Against Exploitation (Articles 23–24)

Human trafficking, forced labour (begar), and child labour in hazardous industries are constitutionally prohibited. No person under 14 years can legally work in factories, mines, or dangerous occupations — violation is a criminal offence. Bonded labour is also unconstitutional.

4. Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25–28)

Every person has the freedom to profess, practice, and propagate any religion. No person can be forced to pay taxes for the religious instruction of another religion. Government-funded schools cannot provide religious instruction.

5. Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29–30)

Minorities have the right to preserve their language, script, and culture. Minority communities — religious and linguistic — have the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice.

6. Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32)

Dr. BR Ambedkar called this "the heart and soul of the Constitution." This is the right to approach the Supreme Court directly for enforcement of fundamental rights. The court can issue writs — habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, certiorari, and quo warranto — to enforce your rights.

👮 Police & Arrest Rights

Your Rights When Arrested — What Police Can and Cannot Do

Many Indians are unaware of the legal protections they have when stopped, questioned, or arrested by police. These rights are enforceable — police violation of these rights can result in legal action against the officer.

📜 RTI Act

Right to Information (RTI) — Ask Any Question to Any Government Office

The Right to Information Act 2005 allows any Indian citizen to formally request information from any government department at the central, state, or local level. The government must respond within 30 days (or 48 hours for matters affecting life and liberty). The filing fee is only ₹10.

What You Can Ask Via RTI

  • Why your ration card, Ayushman card, or pension application was rejected
  • The status of your MGNREGA wages — whether they were released and by whom
  • Copies of government orders, inspection reports, or surveys affecting your area
  • Budget and expenditure records of your Gram Panchayat or municipality
  • Why a government school or hospital is non-functional in your area
  • Land acquisition notices and compensation details
  • Details of beneficiary lists for PM Awas Yojana or any housing scheme

How to File an RTI Application

  1. 1
    Identify the Right DepartmentDetermine which government department holds the information you need. Each ministry and department has a designated Public Information Officer (PIO).
  2. 2
    Write a Simple ApplicationAddress the application to the PIO of the concerned department. State clearly what information you want. No complex language needed — plain Hindi or English works. Use MeraHaq's free RTI Tool to generate your letter.
  3. 3
    Pay ₹10 FeeAttach a ₹10 Indian Postal Order (IPO) or court fee stamp. BPL cardholders are exempt from this fee.
  4. 4
    Submit and Keep a CopySubmit by post or in person and keep a copy with the date stamp. For central government departments, you can also file online at rtionline.gov.in — no fee for online applications.
⚖️ Free Legal Aid

NALSA — Free Legal Help for Every Eligible Indian

Under Article 39A, the state must provide free legal aid to ensure justice is not denied due to poverty. The National Legal Services Authority (NALSA) provides completely free legal services to eligible citizens including: free lawyers for all court cases, legal advice and counselling, help drafting documents and complaints, mediation services, and assistance at Lok Adalats.

Who Is Eligible for Free Legal Aid via NALSA

  • Women and children — regardless of income
  • SC/ST persons
  • Persons with disabilities
  • Victims of trafficking, disasters, or ethnic violence
  • Persons whose annual income is below ₹3 lakh (central government criterion — states may vary)
  • Persons in custody or under trial
  • Industrial workmen
📞 Call NALSA: 15100 The NALSA helpline 15100 provides free guidance on how to access legal aid, where to find your nearest District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) office, and how to file a complaint if your rights are violated. Also visit your nearest District Court's DLSA office directly.

Lok Adalat — Fast, Free, Final Settlement

NALSA organises Lok Adalats (People's Courts) where disputes are settled through mediation — completely free. Settlements at Lok Adalat are final and binding, equivalent to a court decree, and cannot be appealed. They typically resolve cases in one day that would take years in regular courts. They handle motor accident cases, matrimonial disputes (except divorce), labour disputes, consumer cases, and many more.
